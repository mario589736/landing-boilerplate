# Copy Frameworks for Landing Pages

Reference for writing conversion-focused copy. Use these when drafting hero headlines, feature descriptions, CTAs, and email sequences.

---

## PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solve

The most reliable landing page structure.

**Problem:** Name the specific, painful problem your visitor has right now. Be precise. Vague problems create vague conversions.

**Agitate:** Describe what happens if the problem is not solved. Make the cost of inaction real. Do not exaggerate — it kills trust.

**Solve:** Show your product as the clear, credible path out.

**Example:**
- Problem: "Most landing pages take 3-4 weeks to build from scratch."
- Agitate: "That is four weeks of no leads, no proof, no momentum — while your competitors are live."
- Solve: "This boilerplate ships in a day. Design system included."

---

## BAB — Before, After, Bridge

Useful for testimonials, hero subheadlines, and email subjects.

**Before:** The world as it is now (painful).
**After:** The world as it could be (desirable).
**Bridge:** How you get from before to after.

**Example:**
- Before: "You are spending days fighting CSS instead of talking to customers."
- After: "Your landing page is live and converting."
- Bridge: "Clone this repo and deploy in 20 minutes."

---

## Value Proposition Formula

> [Product] helps [specific person] do [specific outcome] without [specific pain/sacrifice].

**Example:** "This boilerplate helps non-designers ship a professional landing page in a week without writing CSS from scratch."

Fill in all four blanks before writing a single headline.

## Headline formulas

Ranked by conversion rate (based on CXL and Copyhackers research):

1. **Outcome headline:** "Ship your landing page this week." (direct, declarative)
2. **Problem headline:** "Stop spending three weeks on setup." (calls out the pain)
3. **How-to headline:** "How to go from idea to live page in a day." (process-oriented)
4. **Social proof headline:** "500+ founders use this to ship faster." (credibility first)

Avoid:
- Question headlines (creates hesitation, not momentum)
- Clever/pun headlines (confuse more than they convert)
- "Welcome to…" (zero value, immediate trust loss)

## CTA copy

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| Submit | Get started |
| Click here | Download now |
| Learn more | See how it works |
| Sign up | Create your free account |
| Contact us | Book a 25-min call |

Rules:
- Start with an action verb.
- Be specific about what happens next.
- One primary CTA per viewport. Do not dilute.
- The micro-copy under the CTA ("No credit card required", "Cancel anytime") dramatically increases clicks.

## Social proof hierarchy

Most to least credible:

1. Specific outcome with numbers ("Reduced build time by 60%")
2. Before/after narrative ("We used to spend weeks on this. Now we ship in a day.")
3. Feature praise from a credible person ("Best design system I've worked with")
4. Vague testimonials ("Great product, highly recommend") — barely better than nothing

Requirements for a credible testimonial:
- Real full name, title, and company (no "John D., CEO")
- Specific outcome or use case
- Photo (increases trust by ~35% per research)

## Objection removal

Every FAQ should address the actual hesitations, not softballs. Common objections:

| Objection | Answer pattern |
|-----------|----------------|
| Price | Frame as ROI, not cost. "€X/month saves you Y hours." |
| Trust | Show logos, testimonials, or press mentions. |
| Complexity | Reduce perceived effort. "Takes 20 minutes to set up." |
| Timing | Create urgency only if real. Fake scarcity kills credibility. |
| Alternatives | Acknowledge competition, differentiate honestly. |

## GDPR note (for EU audiences)

Forms collecting email must include:
- What you will use the email for (no vague "newsletter")
- Link to privacy policy
- Unchecked opt-in checkbox for marketing (cannot be pre-checked)
- Clear unsubscribe mechanism

Do not use dark patterns on consent — the risk-to-trust trade-off is never worth it.
